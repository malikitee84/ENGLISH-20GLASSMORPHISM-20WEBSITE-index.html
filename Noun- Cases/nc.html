Noun Cases in English

Nominative Case (subject case):

Used for the subject of a sentence or clause.

Examples: "I," "he," "she," "they," "we."
Accusative Case (objective case):

Used for the direct object of a verb, the object of a preposition, or the target of an action.

Examples: "me," "him," "her," "them," "us."
Genitive Case (possessive case):

Used to indicate ownership or possession.

Examples: "my," "his," "her," "their," "our."

It's worth noting that the use of noun cases in English is generally limited to pronouns and possessive forms. Modern English has largely simplified its case system compared to older forms of the language, like Old English. In many cases, word order and prepositions are used to convey the relationships that noun cases might convey in other languages.

For example:

  • Nominative: "She sings a song."
  • Accusative: "I saw him at the park."
  • Genitive: "This is Sarah's book."

Overall, while English does have some vestiges of noun cases, its system is much simpler than that of many other languages with more elaborate case systems.
